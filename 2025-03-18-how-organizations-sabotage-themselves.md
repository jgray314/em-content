# How Organizations Sabotage Themselves

Have you ever had a coworker or boss you thought might be trying to sabotage a project or mission?

Get ready to question everyone, including yourself.

Many commonplace office behaviors are listed in the original guide to layperson sabotage, the *Simple Sabotage Field Manual*. Don't judge yourself or your coworkers too harshly — the sabotage suggestions here were painfully common practices back in the 1940s too. Recognizing the harm in these practices is the first step in ending them.

### Process

> Multiply procedures and clearances, require three approvals where one would do.
>
> Multiply paperwork, start duplicate files.

The manual's recommendations for a would-be manager or office-worker saboteur focus at length on process, and on using it to slow work down. The first and most insidious class of these builds delays directly into the process — through duplicated approvals, roles, and efforts — and then enforces those and other processes to the letter in all circumstances. Regular process reviews, and even more importantly, convenient and effective employee feedback loops, can tame this. It's hard to overstate the value of easy pathways for employee feedback and grievances — and the manual explicitly calls for making them as inconvenient as possible.

A second, larger set of suggestions are more tactical ways of slowing processes down:

- Demand slower communication processes.
- Actively prevent resolution of conversations.
- Time responses and delivery to maximize delays.
- Use duplication to make errors or inconsistent information more likely.

Agile development practices provide resilience against these tactics. Daily check-ins and information radiators speed up communication. Timeboxing and a "done is better than perfect" philosophy drive resolution of conversations. Fixed durations and work-in-progress limits provide visibility into delays and patterns of delay. Agile does focus on maintaining a single source of truth, but the broader design principle here is to make the right thing easy and the wrong thing hard. When an error repeats — especially with serious consequences — the problem usually isn't the person, but the system that produced the outcome.

### Decisions

> When possible, refer all matters to committees for "further study and consideration." Attempt to make the committees as large as possible — never less than five.

Slowing the decision-making process received special attention in the manual, and for good reason: slow decisions consume the time of everyone involved and can block the work of a much wider group. The decision-maker as bottleneck remains a common organizational problem today. A modern rewrite of the manual's sabotage recommendations for decisions would read:

- Allow no shortcuts, ever, in decision processes.
- Maximally inflict monologues.
- Refer everything to committees, and make them as large as possible.
- Bikeshed.
- Reopen resolved decisions.
- Create excessive fear and caution around decisions.
- Expand the set of potential blockers for a decision.

This list makes the value of Amazon's Type 1 and Type 2 decision framework clear. It provides a streamlined path for most decisions while ensuring time is spent on the most important and hardest-to-reverse ones. It also underscores the need to keep the number of people involved in a decision low, in order to protect velocity and innovation.

As organizations grow, there's a tendency to apply the heavyweight Type 1 process to most decisions, including many that are really Type 2. The result is slowness, unthoughtful risk aversion, insufficient experimentation, and diminished invention — a pattern Jeff Bezos described directly in his [1997 letter to Amazon shareholders](https://ir.aboutamazon.com/annual-reports-proxies-and-shareholder-letters/default.aspx).

### Management Dysfunction

> Sign out unimportant jobs first, assign important jobs to inefficient workers.
>
> Be pleasant to inefficient workers, give undeserved promotions.
>
> Discriminate against efficient workers, complain unjustly.

The manual reserved a special, more insidious class of recommendations for managers and supervisors: using their role itself to make the organization less effective. Misallocating skills is harmful on its own, but the deeper damage is eroding trust and motivation across the whole team through unfair rewards and complaints. The impact of bad managers is well documented, and there are longstanding practices to detect and prevent it — reporting structures for unfair treatment, regular monitoring and review of manager behavior (especially around promotions and pay), documented leveling frameworks, and employee sentiment surveys. None of it works without a foundation of fair and equitable treatment of employees.

### Training and Development

> When training new workers, give incomplete or misleading instructions.

Training and development are a critical part of making an organization effective, and the manual recognizes this too. Don't sabotage your new employees — give them complete, clear, and up-to-date information. Reward employees who help others ramp up and pass on their skills. Invest in your onboarding materials and experience, and you'll see the return in new employees contributing more, faster.

### So What?

The lessons in the *Simple Sabotage Field Manual* won't answer every organizational problem, but they can point the way to some solutions — or at least give you pause before you sabotage yourself.
