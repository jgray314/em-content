# Lessons from Sabotage

### Learning from Intentional Failure

Sometimes the best way to learn how to do something well is to look at failures. This comes up in many places: accident prevention, disaster preparedness, engineering post mortems. One of my early influences in learning how to be a leader came not from examples of things done well, but from a document on how to intentionally sabotage an organization and get away with it.

During WW2 the OSS, precursor to the CIA, created a guide for everyday people who wanted to resist Axis occupation with minimal risk to themselves: *The Simple Sabotage Field Manual*. While some serious sabotage was included, the document was mostly about "teaching people to do their jobs badly." Much of what it highlights will feel distressingly familiar to workers today. Your co-workers are almost certainly not aiming for sabotage, but the behaviors have the same impact regardless of intent. This slim document is one of the most condensed sources of wisdom on how to support or hinder an effort that I've come across.

### The Hidden Impact of Every Team Member

> A non-cooperative attitude may involve nothing more than creating an unpleasant situation among one's fellow workers, engaging in bickering, or displaying surliness and stupidity.
>
> This type of activity... is frequently responsible for accidents, delays, and general destruction even under normal conditions.

Most efforts depend on a large set of contributors and community members to succeed. Every member of a project, team, or company — from the CEO to the contract office cleaners — can help or hinder the work. This happens through direct actions, like unplugging a computer or withholding a key strategy, but indirect behavior can be just as impactful. Making a workplace difficult or unpleasant harms the efforts of others and tends to compound pre-existing problems. Keeping around someone with poor judgment or a poor attitude saps a team's mission and drives good people away.

### Understanding and Driving Motivation

> Purposeful stupidity is contrary to human nature.

Motivating others is a key challenge for a leader, but rarely does a leader face difficulties as tough as encouraging everyday people to put themselves at risk to resist an occupying force. Most jobs don't ask workers to put themselves in danger. On the whole, people by default want to do a task well and to maintain the objects and relationships around them. Craftsmanship is not a long-gone value — it's a key intrinsic motivator, especially for an effort's most valuable members. Asking someone to compromise on it is asking more than most leaders realize.

The manual offers some useful best practices for motivating people, especially in distributed groups or with individuals who need to act independently:

- Make rewards or benefits for actions as specific as possible, rather than framed as abstract discussions about advancing a value.
- Recognize the efforts you want to see in others regardless of specific success, and highlight the portion of a population that's making the attempt.
- Develop a sense of responsibility across the group to educate and develop others.
- Cultivate a state of mind where everyone feels able to identify potential methods of improvement — particularly important for domain experts.

### Infrastructure: Where Small Problems Become Big Ones

On a long enough timescale, certain emergencies are virtually guaranteed. Take the time to consider your "expected" emergencies, and prepare with plans and mitigations. Ensure the maintenance of core systems and effective training on them — especially around the ill-considered shortcuts that cause harm in the long run. The overwhelming majority of the sabotage examples in the manual are built on inappropriate or slipshod cleaning, maintenance, and use of tools and systems. Manufacturing isn't as common a form of employment as it was in the 1940s, so the specific examples are less relevant today, but most jobs have equivalent practices.

Petty inconvenience adds up, and it matters. A painful, janky travel portal, expense system, payroll process, or commit workflow has sizable hidden costs. This adds up — especially when it happens at an already frustrating time, like during a 3am page or while on business travel. Make it easy to do the right thing by default, and make mistakes easy to catch quickly. Documentation can support this, but it's insufficient on its own and needs to be regularly updated. Along the same lines, communication is critical: make it easy to discover who to contact about a given effort and how to reach them.

### Organizational Friction

The lessons discussed here have so far focused on individual actions. What about when sabotage becomes institutionalized — paralyzing decision-making, stifling innovation, and demoralizing top performers? That's the subject of the companion piece, "How Organizations Sabotage Themselves."
